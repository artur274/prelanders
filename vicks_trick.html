Doctors Baffled After Viral 'Vicks Trick' Video Crashes Supplement Website — Here's What We Found

A homemade remedy video featuring VapoRub and baking soda has amassed millions of views. Our health desk investigates the science — and why one company can't keep their product in stock.

By Sarah Chen, Health Correspondent
Published March 21, 2026 · Updated 2 hours ago · 6 min read
14,832 people reading this article right now

A video that has been circulating on social media for the past 72 hours has sent shockwaves through the men's health community — and crashed at least one supplement company's website in the process.

The clip, which has been viewed an estimated 2.3 million times before being flagged for removal on several platforms, shows a man describing a simple home remedy involving Vicks VapoRub, baking soda, and warm water that he claims dramatically improved blood flow and restored performance for men over 45.

According to the video's narrator, a doctor affiliated with Stanford University allegedly shared a natural method that targets what he describes as "flaccid plaques" — buildup inside blood vessels that restricts circulation to certain areas of the male body. The remedy, he claims, clears these blockages rapidly and forces increased blood flow.

WKRN's health desk reached out to Stanford University for comment. A spokesperson declined to confirm or deny any specific faculty involvement, stating only that "the university does not endorse specific commercial products or home remedies."

"The active compound in Vicks — menthol — is actually a well-documented vasodilator. It opens blood vessels. That part is real science. What's interesting is that someone appears to have taken this concept and developed a concentrated, pharmaceutical-grade formula around it." — Dr. James Whitfield, Board-Certified Urologist, Cleveland Clinic (via phone interview)

Dr. Whitfield, who has not seen the original video but reviewed the scientific claims at our request, said the underlying mechanism — using natural vasodilators to improve blood flow — is not new in medical literature.

"What surprises me is how many of my patients over the last week have come in asking about this," he told WKRN. "At least a dozen men, all over 45, all referencing the same video. Several of them said they've already tried a supplement version and reported significant improvements."

The Supplement Connection

While the viral video describes a DIY approach, viewers were directed to a link below the video that leads to a company selling a concentrated formula based on the same active compounds. That website experienced what appears to be a major traffic surge.

Web analytics firm SimilarWeb shows the company's domain received approximately 840,000 unique visitors in a 48-hour period — a 12,000% increase over its monthly average. The site's server reportedly went down twice, each time for approximately 30 minutes.

"We've never seen anything like this," said a customer service representative who answered our call. "We're shipping out thousands of orders a day. Our warehouse team is working double shifts."

What's Actually In the Formula?

According to the product's label, which we were able to review during a brief window when the site was accessible, the supplement contains a proprietary blend of natural vasodilators and blood-flow enhancers. The company claims these are the same active compounds found in the Vicks remedy — but in concentrated, standardized doses.

🧪 Key Ingredients Listed on the Product Label:

  • Menthol Extract (pharmaceutical-grade, concentrated)
  • L-Arginine — amino acid shown to boost nitric oxide production
  • Tongkat Ali Root — traditional vitality herb, clinically studied
  • Maca Root Extract — linked to improved energy and stamina
  • Zinc & Magnesium Complex — essential minerals for male health

Dr. Whitfield noted that several of these ingredients have peer-reviewed research supporting their effects on vascular health and male performance.

"L-Arginine alone has over 30 clinical studies behind it. Combined with menthol's vasodilating properties, I can see why men are reporting rapid results. The science isn't magic — it's blood flow." — Dr. James Whitfield, Board-Certified Urologist

Why Is the Video Being Removed?

Multiple social media platforms have flagged or removed the original video, citing violations of community guidelines around health claims. This has led to an online backlash, with users arguing the video contains no harmful content.

"They're taking it down because Big Pharma doesn't want you to know about this," wrote one commenter on a reposted version that has since also been removed. While WKRN cannot verify these claims, the pattern of removal has only increased public interest in the video's content.
