Menthol and Circulation: What Science Actually Says About Topical Vasodilators

With viral home remedy videos flooding social media, our health desk examines the real research behind menthol, essential oils, and their effects on blood flow.
By Sarah Chen, Health Correspondent · Published April 2, 2026 · 7 min read

Over the past several months, a wave of viral videos has claimed that common household products containing menthol can be used as home remedies for a variety of health concerns, particularly those related to blood circulation. These videos have attracted millions of views and sparked widespread discussion about the potential therapeutic properties of topical ingredients most people associate with cold relief.

But how much of this is grounded in real science, and where does the evidence end and speculation begin? Our health desk reviewed the available medical literature to separate fact from fiction.

What Is Menthol and How Does It Work?

Menthol is a naturally occurring compound found in mint plants, particularly peppermint and cornmint. It has been used in medicine for centuries, primarily as a topical analgesic and decongestant. Products like VapoRub, muscle creams, and cough drops all contain menthol as an active ingredient.

From a pharmacological perspective, menthol activates cold-sensitive receptors in the skin known as TRPM8 channels. This activation produces the familiar cooling sensation and can also trigger localized changes in blood vessel diameter.

Menthol is classified as a peripheral vasodilator in pharmacological literature, meaning it can cause blood vessels near the surface of the skin to widen temporarily when applied topically.

Source: Journal of Pharmacy and Pharmacology, Review of Topical Analgesic Mechanisms

The Science of Topical Vasodilation

Vasodilation, the widening of blood vessels, is a normal physiological process that plays an important role in regulating blood pressure, body temperature, and tissue oxygenation. Several natural compounds are known to promote vasodilation through different mechanisms:

  • Menthol activates TRPM8 receptors, causing localized vessel relaxation and the characteristic cooling sensation on the skin
  • Capsaicin, found in hot peppers, stimulates TRPV1 receptors and produces warming and increased superficial blood flow
  • Camphor, often combined with menthol in topical products, has mild vasodilatory and anti-inflammatory properties
  • Eucalyptol, derived from eucalyptus oil, has shown potential to improve local circulation in preliminary studies

It is important to understand that the vasodilatory effects of topical menthol are primarily local, meaning they affect blood vessels in the immediate area where the product is applied. The effects are temporary and typically subside as the compound is absorbed or evaporates from the skin.

What the Research Actually Shows

Several published studies have examined the cardiovascular effects of menthol and related compounds. A 2018 study in the American Journal of Physiology found that menthol application increased forearm blood flow in healthy volunteers, confirming its local vasodilatory effect. However, the researchers noted that these effects were modest and transient.

Research on dietary and supplemental compounds that support nitric oxide production, such as L-arginine and dietary nitrates from vegetables like beetroot, has a more robust evidence base for systemic cardiovascular effects. These compounds work through different pathways than topical menthol and have been studied more extensively in clinical settings.

Key distinction: Topical vasodilation (from menthol applied to the skin) and systemic vasodilation (from dietary compounds that increase nitric oxide production) are fundamentally different processes. Topical application primarily affects superficial blood vessels at the site of application, while dietary approaches can have broader effects on the cardiovascular system.

The Role of Nitric Oxide

Much of the scientific interest in natural vasodilators centers on nitric oxide, a molecule that plays a critical role in vascular health. The body produces nitric oxide from dietary precursors, and it serves as a signaling molecule that tells blood vessel walls to relax and widen.

Compounds that support nitric oxide production have been studied for their potential effects on blood pressure, exercise performance, and cardiovascular health. These include L-arginine, L-citrulline, and dietary nitrates found in leafy greens and root vegetables.

The connection between nitric oxide and vascular health is well established in medical literature. However, the leap from established science to the dramatic claims made in viral social media videos requires scrutiny and critical thinking.

Why Viral Health Claims Require Caution

The popularity of home remedy videos highlights a broader challenge in health communication. While it is positive that people are interested in understanding how their bodies work, viral content often oversimplifies complex science, omits important safety considerations, or extrapolates far beyond what the evidence supports.

Safety note: Applying concentrated menthol products or essential oils to sensitive areas of the body can cause chemical burns, skin irritation, and allergic reactions. Many common household products containing menthol are formulated for specific uses such as chest application for congestion relief and should only be used as directed on their labeling.

Health professionals consistently advise against using products in ways not intended by their manufacturers. If you are experiencing health concerns related to circulation or any other issue, the appropriate first step is always to consult with a qualified healthcare provider who can evaluate your individual situation.

The Bottom Line

Menthol is a real compound with documented pharmacological effects, including localized vasodilation. The science behind natural vasodilators and nitric oxide production is legitimate and the subject of ongoing research. However, the gap between these established facts and the claims made in viral social media videos is significant.

Consumers should approach viral health content with healthy skepticism, seek information from reputable medical sources, and consult healthcare professionals before attempting any home remedies or starting new supplements, especially for concerns related to cardiovascular health.

As with most health topics, the truth is more nuanced than a short video can capture. The human body is complex, and solutions that sound too simple to be true usually are.
